Setting up the Pieces

The board setup is shown. On the second and seventh ranks, both sides will have eight pawns. On the back rank, both sides have two knights, two bishops, two rooks, as well as a king and a queen set up as shown. The king must be on the vertical file marked "e" and the queen must be on the file marked "d"

White moves first, and both players alternate turns after that. Only one move can be made at a time, and players can capture enemy pieces that cross the movement path of their own pieces, with the exception of the pawn. To capture a piece, you cannot "jump over" pieces with the exception of the knight.

Moving the Pawns

Pawns move forward and capture other pieces one square diagonally. If a pawn has not moved before in the game, it will be able to advance two squares for one move. In the diagram to the left, the white pawn has the choice to advance either one square or two squares. Because the black pawn is not on its starting square, it will only be able to advance one square.

Moving the Knights

The knight's movement can be described as being in an "L" shape. As shown in the diagram, even if the knight is "blocked" by other pieces, it can still jump over them to its destination.

Moving the Bishops

Bishops move diagonally. These pieces can cover a wide range of squares, especially when placed in the center of the board. In contrast to other pieces however, bishops are only able to move to half of the squares on the board. A bishop on a light square can only control white squares, and a bishop on a dark square can only control dark squares.

Moving the Rooks

Rooks move both vertically and horizontally. These pieces are very fast, but usually do not see active play until the later stages of the game, as more pawns are lost and lines begin to open.

Moving the Queen

The queen has the movement of both the rook and the bishop, combined into one piece. It is the strongest piece, which also means it is more easy to attack than other pieces. Generally, bringing the queen out into the open early in the game is not advised, since enemy pieces can easily threaten to capture it!

Moving the King

The king is the most valuable piece. It moves one square in any direction. The objective of chess is to checkmate the enemy king, which is a situation where the king will not be able to escape capture on the next move. The loss of the king means the loss of the game.

Check

A check is an attack on a king. In the diagram on the left, black's queen has just moved to a position where it threatens to capture white's king on the next move. A check must be responded to, by moving the king, capturing the piece delivering the check, or interposing a piece to block the check.

Checkmate

This position is an example of a checkmate. Black's king has no safe squares to move to where it won't be under attack by an enemy piece, and most importantly, the black king is also in check by white's knight. Additionally, black has no way of removing the attack on their king, so white wins.

Stalemate

Another possible outcome of the game is a stalemate. In the diagram, white has just moved their queen to the position shown. Similar to checkmate, black has no safe moves with the king. The difference here is that the black king is not in check. Since black has no other pieces except the king, the game is a stalemate because black has no legal moves, but is also not in checkmate. Thus, the game ends in a draw. Stalemate almost always happens, when most of the pieces have been captured and taken off the board.

Castling

Castling is a move reserved for the king and rooks. It is the only time in chess when two pieces can move in one turn, and it requires for both the king and the rook that is being castled with to have not moved at all in the game. Additionally, the king must not be castling into a check or through check. If white chooses to castle kingside in the diagram, the king moves to the right two squares and the rook moves to the square circled with red. Queenside castling on the other hand would mean the king moves three squares to the left and the rook moves to the square circled with blue.

En Passant

The last rule you absolutely need to know about is En Passant. In the diagram, black has just pushed a pawn two squares to stand adjacent to white's pawn. On this move and this move only, white has the option to capture black's pawn as if black's pawn had only moved one square. If white chooses to not capture the pawn, they will not be able to capture that pawn in the future.

And that's it! You have now learned how to play chess!
